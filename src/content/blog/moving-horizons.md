### The Lucky Break of 2018
Eight years ago, I walked out of a three-year Computer Science degree and into my first professional role. Despite the academic background, I arrived with exactly one module of HTML/CSS and just enough JavaScript to be dangerous. I didn't have a polished portfolio, a library of side projects, or a deep understanding of the hundreds of tools that define the modern web. 

I lucked into a role where "potential" was a valid currency. I was given the space to move from a basic CMS into the deep end of **Angular**, **TypeScript**, and **RxJS**. I learned on the job because, back then, the industry still had room for juniors to grow into engineers. 

But looking at the landscape in 2026, I have to ask: **If the 2018 version of me applied for a job today, would I even get a screening call?**

The honest answer is almost certainly "No."

### The Complexity Explosion
In less than a decade, the "Frontend" has transitioned from a styling layer to a full-stack orchestration layer. As I moved through my career, the goalposts didn't just move; they accelerated. 

Within a few years, the "entry level" requirements shifted. It wasn't enough to render a page; you had to master **Hydration**, **Server-Side Rendering (SSR)**, and **Micro-frontend architectures**. Our toolkits expanded from simple text editors to a dizzying array of enterprise essentials:

* **The Framework Carousel:** Moving from plain JS to a world where you are expected to be conversational in **Angular**, **React**, **Svelte**, and **Vue**—often simultaneously.
* **The Ecosystem:** Mastering npm, Webpack, and eventually Vite.
* **The Workflow:** Figma for design, LaunchDarkly for feature flagging, and ServiceNow for enterprise flow.
* **The Infrastructure:** Taking ownership of CI/CD pipelines and Azure/DevOps environments.

### The Moving Horizon
Today, we’ve created a "Moving Horizon" for newcomers. No matter how much a student learns, the industry adds three more "essential" requirements by the time they graduate. I’ve watched friends spend years of their lives dedicated to learning development, building complex projects, and studying deep architecture, only to *barely* be considered for a junior role.

That shouldn't be the goal. We’ve turned a creative craft into a marathon where the finish line is pulled further away every mile. By demanding that every developer be a "System Architect-lite" before they've even pushed their first professional commit, we are making the landscape unappealing and, for many, entirely unobtainable.

### Why This Portfolio Exists Now
This brings me to this website. It took me eight years to build an independent portfolio because, for eight years, I was too busy keeping up with this shift. I was busy building bespoke loyalty platforms for the **Premier League** and the **NFL**, or standardizing UI libraries for global retail giants like **Reiss** and **Victoria’s Secret**.

I built this site with **Astro** and **TypeScript** as a return to form. After nearly a decade of navigating framework bloat and architectural complexity, it felt right to build something fast, accessible, and intentional.

### A Call for "Room"
The frontend is more powerful than it has ever been, but it has never been more gate-kept. As we continue to add more tools, more frameworks, and more "essential" skills to the list, we need to ensure we aren't making the entry price too high. The industry shouldn't just be for those who started eight years ago—it needs to remain a space where a curious mind can still find a way in without having to spend years just to reach the starting line.
